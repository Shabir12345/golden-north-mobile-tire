It's a specific kind of Toronto morning. Minus twenty, scraper in hand, already running late, and the car answers the key with a slow, groaning crank that fades into *click-click-click*. The battery was fine yesterday. It was fine all summer. So why did it choose the coldest morning of the year to quit?

Because that's exactly how car batteries fail. The short answer: **near -18°C, a battery delivers only about half the cranking power it produces on a warm day, while the cold engine, full of thickened oil, demands more than usual to turn over.** A strong battery shrugs that off. A weak one hits the gap between what's asked and what it can give, and the first deep freeze is where the two lines cross.

## What cold actually does to a battery

A car battery is a chemistry set: lead plates in acid, producing current through a chemical reaction. Like most chemical reactions, it runs slower when it's cold.

- **Around freezing (0°C)**, a typical battery is already down to roughly two-thirds of its rated strength.
- **Near -18°C** (an ordinary January morning in the GTA) it's producing **about half** its cranking power.
- Meanwhile the engine has gotten harder to start. Cold oil is thicker, so the starter motor needs *more* current to spin the engine, right when the battery has less to offer.

Add the winter accessory load (rear defroster, heated seats, headlights on for the dark commute, fan on full) and the battery is being asked for peak performance under its worst conditions.

## The counterintuitive part: summer killed it, winter just found out

Here's what most drivers get backwards. Cold is when batteries die, but it's rarely what kills them. **The real damage is done in summer.** Heat evaporates fluid inside the battery and corrodes the plates, quietly, gradually, with no symptoms. A battery can lose a big share of its capacity over a hot July and August and still start the car every morning, because a mild-weather start only needs a fraction of its strength.

Then the first -15°C morning arrives, demand doubles, supply halves, and the weakness that summer created finally shows. That's why the tow trucks are busy in January but the damage happened in July, and why a battery that "seemed fine in October" was never actually fine.

## How long should a battery last here?

In the Canadian cycle of hot summers and hard winters, **three to five years** is the realistic range. Where you land in it depends on how the car lives:

- **Lots of short trips** age a battery faster. Starting the engine takes a large bite of charge, and a ten-minute run to the store doesn't repay it. Do that all winter and the battery slides slowly downhill.
- **Parking outdoors** through January is harder on it than a garage. Even an unheated one takes the edge off the deepest cold.
- **A car that sits for weeks** (a downtown condo car, used twice a month) discharges on its own, and modern cars keep drawing a small current even when parked.

If your battery is past four years old, treat every winter as a bonus round it may not finish.

## Warning signs a battery gives before it quits

Batteries almost always telegraph the failure. In the weeks before a no-start, watch for:

- **A slow, labouring crank**: the engine turning over noticeably lazier than usual, especially on cold mornings
- **Dashboard and headlights dimming** at the moment of startup
- **Needing more than one boost in a season**: a battery that keeps needing rescue is telling you something
- **The battery warning light** flickering on, which can also point to the alternator
- **Visible trouble under the hood**: crusty blue-white corrosion on the terminals, or a battery case that looks swollen

Any one of these in November is worth a load test. A test takes minutes and tells you exactly how much strength the battery has left, quick certainty against a no-start in an underground garage at -20°C.

## How to help your battery survive the winter

None of this is complicated, and all of it works:

1. **Get the battery tested before winter**, ideally in October or November. A load test catches the weak battery in your driveway instead of a parking lot in January.
2. **Give the car one good drive a week**: twenty to thirty minutes at steady speed lets the alternator genuinely recharge what all those short trips took out.
3. **Switch off the heater fan, rear defrost, and radio before shutting the engine off**, so the next start spends everything on starting.
4. **Keep the terminals clean.** Corrosion adds resistance right where you can least afford it; a wire brush and a rinse of baking-soda water fixes it in five minutes.
5. **Park indoors when you can.** Even a few degrees warmer at rest means measurably more cranking power at 7 a.m.

## Boost, or replace?

An honest answer, because they solve different problems. **A boost fixes a flat battery; it doesn't fix a bad one.** If the battery drained because a door sat ajar overnight, a boost and a proper drive may be the end of the story. But if a healthy-looking morning still produced a slow crank, or the battery is four-plus years old, a boost only postpones the same failure to a colder, less convenient morning.

The way to know is to test rather than guess: check the battery's actual output under load, and check that the charging system is refilling it properly. That's exactly how we approach every battery call.
