A tire rarely fails politely. At 100 km/h on the 401 it's a bang you feel through the floor, then a low flapping drone and a steering wheel that suddenly wants to go somewhere you don't. What you do in the next sixty seconds matters far more than the flat itself — because on a busy highway, the danger was never the tire. It's the traffic passing a metre from your door.

Here's the short version, then the detail behind each step:

1. **Don't brake hard or swerve.** Grip the wheel, ease off the gas, let the car slow on its own.
2. **Signal and work your way to the right shoulder** — past the rumble strip, as far from the lane as the pavement allows.
3. **Hazards on immediately.** At night, leave your parking lights on too.
4. **Stay buckled inside** unless you can wait behind a guardrail well away from traffic.
5. **Call for help** with your highway, direction, and nearest exit ready.

## How do you know it's a flat and not something else?

A blowout announces itself: a pop or bang, then a rhythmic flap-flap-flap that speeds up with the car. A slow leak is sneakier — the car starts pulling gently to one side, the steering feels heavy or vague, or your tire-pressure warning light comes on. Either way, the response is the same. Don't stab the brakes to "check." Sudden inputs on a deflating tire are how a nuisance becomes a spin.

## The first thirty seconds: keep the car straight

A flat tire drags the car toward its own corner, and your instincts — brake hard, steer away — are exactly wrong. Instead:

- **Hold the wheel firmly with both hands.** Expect a pull and correct it smoothly.
- **Lift off the accelerator and let speed bleed away gradually.** No hard braking until the car is stable and slow.
- **Signal early** and start easing right, one lane at a time. GTA drivers will let you in when your hazards are flashing; nobody wants to be behind a car on three tires.

If the flat happens in the express lanes of the 401, don't panic about crossing to the collectors — just keep moving right at whatever pace traffic allows and take the next transfer or exit.

## Where should you stop?

The goal is maximum distance between your car and live traffic, on straight, flat, firm ground:

- **Past the rumble strip and as far right as the shoulder allows.** Angle the wheels slightly right, so if the car is struck it moves away from you, not into the lane.
- **Avoid stopping on curves, crests, bridges, or under overpasses** where approaching drivers can't see you until late.
- **An exit ramp, onramp gore, carpool lot, or wide verge beats a bare shoulder** every time. If one is within a kilometre, crawl to it with your hazards on.

And here's the permission most guides won't give you: **it's okay to ruin the tire.** Some stretches of the 401 through Toronto — especially the collectors — have shoulders barely wider than a mirror. If there's nowhere safe to stop, drive slowly on the flat to the next exit. A destroyed tire and a scuffed rim are a repair bill. A car stopped half in a live lane is a collision waiting for a distracted driver.

## Make yourself visible

Once stopped, your job is to be seen from as far away as possible:

- **Hazard lights on**, always, immediately.
- **At night or in rain**, leave the parking lights on as well.
- **If you carry reflective triangles or flares** and can place them without walking near traffic, set them 30 metres or more behind the car — far enough that drivers react before they reach you. If placing them means standing on the traffic side, skip it. The triangles aren't worth it.

## Should you change it yourself?

Sometimes, honestly, yes. In a rest stop, a carpool lot, or on a quiet regional road with the flat on the passenger side, a confident driver with a good jack can be rolling again in twenty minutes.

But say no to the DIY change if **any** of these is true:

- The flat is on the **traffic side** of the car
- The shoulder is **narrow** or traffic is passing close and fast
- It's **dark, raining, or snowing** and you'd be hard to see
- The ground is **soft, sloped, or uneven** — jacks fail on bad ground
- You'd be relying on the **space-saver spare and wrench you've never used**

There's no prize for changing a tire beside 120 km/h traffic. Professionals who work highway shoulders position a truck with warning lights between themselves and traffic for a reason — you don't have that truck. It is completely fine to stay buckled and make a phone call instead. That's not giving up; that's the correct read of the situation.

## What to have ready when you call

Help finds you faster when you can answer four questions:

1. **Which highway and direction** — "401 eastbound," "DVP southbound."
2. **The nearest exit, interchange, or landmark** — the last exit you passed works; so do the small green kilometre markers posted along 400-series highways.
3. **Your vehicle** — make, model, and colour, so the driver spots you on a shoulder full of cars.
4. **Which tire is flat** — it tells the operator how the truck should position when it arrives.

## While you wait

- **Stay buckled inside with the doors locked** — a shoulder is still part of a highway, and your car's structure and seatbelt protect you better than standing beside it.
- **If a guardrail or barrier sits well off the road** and you can reach it through the passenger side without crossing traffic, waiting behind it is a good option, especially if traffic is passing very close.
- **Never stand directly behind or in front of the car**, and don't try to flag down passing vehicles.
- If anyone stops and something feels off, stay in the car and crack the window to talk. A dispatched driver will know your name, your vehicle, and why they're there.

## Lower the odds of the next one

Most highway flats start days earlier as slow leaks. A monthly two-minute check is the simplest roadside assistance there is:

- **Check pressures monthly** (including the spare) when the tires are cold — the correct numbers are on the sticker inside the driver's door jamb, not on the tire.
- **Do the quarter test**: insert a quarter into the tread with the caribou's nose pointing down. If you can see the tip of its nose, the tread is getting thin.
- **Don't ignore a tire that needs topping up every week.** That's a puncture or a leaking valve, and it will pick highway speed and a cold night to finish the job.
