Every year it's the same story across the GTA: the first real snowfall lands on a Tuesday night, and by Wednesday morning every tire shop from Oshawa to Oakville has a two-week waiting list. The drivers stuck in that queue made one understandable mistake: they waited for snow, when the thing that actually matters is temperature.

The rule worth remembering: **switch to winter tires once daytime temperatures stay consistently below 7°C.** In the GTA, that's usually late October to mid-November, weeks before the first flake. Keep them on until spring temperatures hold above 7°C, typically mid-April.

## Why 7°C, and not the first snowfall?

It comes down to what tires are made of. Every tire is a compromise between grip and durability, tuned to a temperature range:

- **All-season rubber** is blended for mild weather. Below roughly 7°C it begins to stiffen, and hard rubber grips pavement the way a hockey puck grips ice. Your tires can be losing traction on a perfectly dry, frosty morning in early November, no snow in sight.
- **Winter rubber** is a softer compound that stays flexible in the cold, so it keeps conforming to the road surface. Add deeper tread and thousands of tiny slits called sipes that bite into snow and slush, and you get a tire built for the actual conditions of a Toronto winter: cold and dry most days, ugly some days.

That's the part most drivers miss: winter tires aren't snow tires. Snow is the bonus case. The everyday case is grip and stopping distance on cold pavement, and it's why testing by Transport Canada and tire manufacturers consistently shows winter tires shortening braking distances on cold and snowy surfaces, in some conditions by as much as 25 per cent. At city speeds that can be the difference between a close call at a Yonge Street crosswalk and something much worse.

## The 7-for-7 rule of thumb

If "consistently below 7°C" feels vague, the CAA suggests a simple version: **seven consecutive days below 7°C means it's time.** Watch the forecast in mid-October. When you see a solid week of single-digit highs lined up, book your changeover. You'll beat the snow and the rush that follows it.

## What the calendar looks like in the GTA

Ontario is a big province, and timing shifts with geography:

- **Toronto and the GTA:** temperatures usually settle below 7°C between **late October and mid-November**. That's your window.
- **Cottage country and northern Ontario:** earlier, often early-to-mid October. If you commute north on the 400 or drive to Muskoka through the fall, err on the early side.
- **Spring removal:** once daytime highs hold above 7°C, **typically mid-April** in the GTA. Don't run winters into warm weather; the soft compound that saves you in January wears down fast on hot pavement, and you'll be buying a new set sooner than you should.

## Are winter tires required by law in Ontario?

No, and this surprises people who've driven in other provinces. **Quebec requires winter tires on all passenger vehicles from December 1 to March 15.** British Columbia requires them on most highways outside the Lower Mainland from October 1. Ontario only recommends them.

But Ontario does put money behind the recommendation: **since January 2016, insurers here have been required to offer a discount to drivers who install four winter tires.** It's often in the range of a few per cent off your premium. Not fortune-changing, but it quietly offsets part of the changeover cost every single year. If you've never asked your insurer about it, ask. Many drivers are leaving that discount on the table.

## Check for the mountain snowflake, not "M+S"

Two markings on a sidewall look reassuring, and only one means anything in a Canadian winter:

- **M+S (Mud and Snow)** is a tread-pattern designation with no cold-weather testing behind it. Most all-seasons carry it. It does not make a tire a winter tire.
- **The three-peak mountain snowflake (3PMSF)** is the one that counts: it certifies the tire met a recognized snow-traction standard. Dedicated winter tires carry it, and so do proper **all-weather** tires.

All-weathers deserve an honest word: for a downtown driver who parks in a condo garage, drives short distances, and can leave the car on the worst days, a quality all-weather with the snowflake symbol is a legitimate one-set compromise, and it usually still qualifies for the insurance discount. For anyone commuting the 401 or 404 daily through January, a dedicated winter set is still the better tool.

## Beat the rush, and think about rims

The busiest week of the year for every tire installer in the GTA is the week after the first real snowfall. Booking in late October means choosing your time slot; booking after the first storm means taking whatever's left.

One decision that pays off every year: **keep your winter tires mounted on their own set of rims.** The seasonal swap becomes a straightforward wheel change instead of dismounting and remounting rubber twice a year: faster, gentler on the tires, and less expensive over the life of the set. Steel rims are the budget-friendly route, and they take winter's salt and potholes so your alloys don't have to.
