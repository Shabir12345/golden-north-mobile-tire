It's a familiar Toronto moment: one tire picks up a drywall screw on the Gardiner, the puncture is in the sidewall so it can't be repaired, and the first quote you get is for a full set of four — for a car with three perfectly good tires on it.

This is exactly the situation the new-versus-used question exists for, so here's the short answer up front: **buy new when you're replacing a full set you'll run for years — you get maximum tread life, current rubber technology, and a warranty. Choose a quality used tire when you're matching a single damaged one, the vehicle is near the end of its life, or the budget needs a bridge, not a mortgage.**

Both answers are right in different situations. The rest of this guide is about knowing which situation you're in — and how to tell a good used tire from a liability, because both absolutely exist.

## The honest case against used tires

Let's start with the criticism, because it's fair. Consumer Reports has long advised against buying used tires for one core reason: **you don't know where a tire has been.** It could have been run overloaded, chronically underinflated, or repaired badly — and some of that damage hides inside the tire's structure where no glance will find it.

That warning is a real one, and it's aimed at a real thing: the pile of loose tires at the back of a lot, sold as-is with no inspection and no standards. Buying from that pile is a gamble, and we'd tell you not to take it.

But "never buy used" also throws out a lot of genuinely good rubber. Tires come off cars for reasons that have nothing to do with the tire — leases end, cars get written off in fender-benders, drivers upsize their wheels. The difference between a bargain and a hazard isn't luck. It's whether anyone with standards actually inspected the tire. Which brings us to the checklist.

## What a used tire has to pass before it's worth buying

Whether you're buying from us or anywhere else, a used tire should clear **every** bar on this list — one failure and it's a no:

- **Tread depth of at least 4/32"**, measured with a gauge, not eyeballed. The legal minimum is 2/32", but wet grip falls off well before that — 4/32" is where a used tire still has real life to give.
- **Even wear across the whole tread.** A tire worn bald on one edge spent its life misaligned or underinflated, and that history follows it.
- **Sound sidewalls** — no bubbles, no bulges, no deep cracking. A sidewall bubble is broken internal cords, and it's a blowout on a timer.
- **No exposed cords or wire** anywhere.
- **No plugs or patches in the shoulder zone.** A proper puncture repair in the centre of the tread is acceptable; a repair near the sidewall is not, because that area flexes too much to hold one safely.
- **An acceptable date code.** Rubber ages even in storage. Find the DOT code on the sidewall — the last four digits are the week and year of manufacture, so **3524 means week 35 of 2024**. Past roughly six years, walk away no matter how deep the tread looks.

Every used tire we sell is inspected against exactly that bar before it goes anywhere near your car. If it wouldn't go on our own vehicles, it doesn't go on yours.

## When a used tire is the smart buy

- **You're replacing one damaged tire, not four.** This is the classic case. A screw through the sidewall shouldn't cost you a full set when the other three tires have plenty of life. The right move is a used tire matched closely in tread depth — and ideally brand and model — to the three staying on the car.
- **One important caveat: all-wheel drive.** AWD systems expect all four tires to be nearly the same diameter. A big tread-depth mismatch makes the tires spin at different rates and can strain the drivetrain — a far more expensive problem than tires. On an AWD vehicle, measure the remaining tread first; if your other three are half-worn, a half-worn matching tire is *better* than a new one.
- **The car is near the end of its road.** Putting a set of premium new tires on a vehicle with two winters left in it is spending on tread the car won't live to use. Matched used tires carry it out properly for a fraction of the cost.
- **You need to be safe *this week* and flush *next month*.** A good used tire today beats a bald tire staying on the car while you save up for new. Budget reality is allowed to be part of a safety decision.

## When new is the better buy

- **You're replacing the full set.** Buying four, you want them starting even, at full depth, wearing predictably together — that's the whole value of a set, and only new gives it to you.
- **It's a winter set you'll run for years.** Winter tires live or die by tread depth and compound freshness; starting at full tread means more seasons per dollar, and current winter compounds genuinely outperform what was on the market even a handful of years ago.
- **You keep your cars a long time.** Tread life, warranty coverage, and known history all pay out over years of ownership. Used tires save money up front; new tires cost less per season when you'll actually use all the seasons.
- **You want the warranty and the certainty.** A new tire's history starts with you. For some drivers that peace of mind alone settles the question, and that's a perfectly good reason.

## Before you call anyone: two things on your sidewall

Two pieces of information make any tire conversation faster:

1. **Your size**, printed on the sidewall and on the sticker inside the driver's door jamb — a code like **225/65R17** (width in millimetres, sidewall ratio, rim diameter in inches).
2. **Your date codes**, if you're assessing the tires you have — last four digits of the DOT number, week and year.

Tell us the size and what happened, and we can usually tell you on the phone whether you're a one-used-tire fix or shopping for a set.
